Design Thinking

In order to create GUIDA, our solution to challenge 2, we utilized the core aspects of design thinking in our creation process to their fullest extent. Design thinking encompasses principles that outline the design process, aiming to innovate and create solutions for people that are both desirable, feasible, and viable. Below you will find how we worked with design thinking under these principles.

Desirability

In order to innovate, we first must understand who our users are. Therefore, we began our design process by formulating our target users which led us to the conclusion that the problem we faced was multifaceted. The stakeholders who formulated the original problem were marketing and management staff at IKEA, but in their view, the issue also affected in-store staff. We settled on designing for both of these groups, focusing on the staff using the product.

One of the key aspects of TEK830 is the use of interviews as a way of information gathering and concretization of user needs. (Coaching Session Sep 12 - Design Thinking). Through interviews with in-store personnel at two IKEA locations we gathered their experiences using the available systems for presenting and selling products. We learned that there exists multiple systems with different purposes. However, they are used in parallel for the sole purpose of answering questions and selling. Furthermore, we found that the knowledge concerning product specifications and relevant logistics already exists in these parallel systems which led us to shift our focus to the unseen and unspoken problem. Therefore, we concluded that the problem IKEA management is experiencing is an issue at a higher level than the physical locations.

In summary, we believe that a combination of the problems the in-store staff, marketing, and management were experiencing would accurately describe the problem statement provided and guide us to a solution that removes knowledge loss and provides easy access to information in an intuitive way. We had an idea that was desired by our users.

Feasibility

The technology required for our solution to function both exists and is readily available. We have planned out the technological components needed to create GUIDA and how we are going to obtain them as well as how we plan to use these tools.

Implementing this into IKEA’s ecosystem is feasible since it requires little to no import of existing IKEA data. Instead, all that is needed is an account and the product is ready to be used. When designing GUIDA we didn’t want to create a solution that would replace existing IKEA systems, since that wouldn’t be realistic for IKEA to implement. This rather serves as a complement to existing IKEA systems, to synergize and make members of the IKEA team work more efficiently.

Viability

The viability of a product can be described as the possibility of profit and long term returns from a solution. (Lecture 4 - Design Thinking) We believe that there are profitable aspects of GUIDA. Firstly, the original problem statement from IKEA comes from a view of lacking efficiency in their value chain. Knowledge loss constitutes major costs in the form of wasted experiences and suboptimal operations. If the combined knowledge in a massive organization such as IKEA could be harvested and consolidated, there would surely be profit to be made.

Considering that GUIDA is aimed to be a standalone product used in parallel with existing systems we believe that the long term returns and maintainability of such a product are highly viable. Integration with existing systems requires a higher level of system and business operations knowledge within IKEA. Integration also leads to a higher cost of development and imposes a risk of failure across systems. Therefore, when designing GUIDA we placed a large focus on creating it as a separate product minimizing these risks and keeping the knowledge required to develop and maintain GUIDA at a minimum. Long term this allows the life-cycle of GUIDA to be placed in the hands of many different teams over time.
